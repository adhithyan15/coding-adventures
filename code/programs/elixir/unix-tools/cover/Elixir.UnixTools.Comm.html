3 comm -- compare two sorted files line by line.
4
5 ## What This Program Does
6
7 This is a reimplementation of the GNU `comm` utility in Elixir. It reads
8 two sorted files and produces three-column output:
9
10 - **Column 1**: Lines unique to file 1.
11 - **Column 2**: Lines unique to file 2.
12 - **Column 3**: Lines common to both files.
13
14 ## How comm Works
15
16 Given two sorted files:
17
18 file1: file2:
19 apple banana
20 banana cherry
21 cherry date
22
23 The output of `comm file1 file2` is:
24
25 apple
26 banana
27 cherry
28 (no common-only lines shown with tabs)
29
30 Wait, let's be precise. Both files share "banana" and "cherry":
31
32 apple (only in file1)
33 \t\tbanana (in both)
34 \t\tcherry (in both)
35 \tdate (only in file2)
36
37 ## Column Suppression
38
39 - `-1`: Suppress column 1 (lines unique to file 1).
40 - `-2`: Suppress column 2 (lines unique to file 2).
41 - `-3`: Suppress column 3 (lines common to both).
42
43 These can be combined: `comm -12 file1 file2` shows only lines in both files.
44
45 ## The Merge Algorithm
46
47 Since both inputs are sorted, we use a merge-style comparison:
48
49 1. Compare the current line from each file.
50 2. If file1's line < file2's line: it's unique to file1 (column 1).
51 3. If file1's line > file2's line: it's unique to file2 (column 2).
52 4. If equal: it's common (column 3).
53 5. Advance the pointer(s) accordingly.
54
55 This is O(n + m) where n and m are the line counts of each file.
56 """
